Mission

Our mission is to use technology and teamwork to protect sharks worldwide. By tracking their movements and using satellite data, we create tools that help conserve sharks and keep ocean ecosystems healthy.

To create a future where sharks thrive and ocean ecosystems stay balanced, using innovation and data-driven conservation.

We started by exploring satellite data and tracking technology to understand shark movements and help protect them globally.

About Us

Interdisciplinary teamwork: Bridges marine biology, data science, and technology to address a global environmental challenge. Scalable framework: The model and database can be adapted to other marine species or regions. Visualization tools: Interactive maps and a web platform make the project accessible and educational for researchers and the public.

Data availability and updates: Delays or interruptions in satellite missions could limit data continuity. Environmental variability: Rapid climate changes may alter patterns faster than models can adapt. Funding and resource limitations: Computational resources and partnerships are needed for project expansion.

Model

Our model creates a database that includes factors that may explain why shark species migrate. Since the data depends on time, we use statistical models for prediction. During data collection, we combine these factors by coordinates and date. For missing values (NaNs), we use K-means to fill them, preparing the data for the model. Next, we perform exploratory data analysis (EDA) to check correlations between variables. As a baseline, we apply regression algorithms such as linear regression, logistic regression, random forest regressor, and SARIMA to confirm the seasonality in the data.

Data Map

Our dataset includes variables such as chlorophyll, pH, sea surface temperature (SST), salinity, and ocean currents. All these attributes are recorded with a date, latitude, and longitude.

During our research on factors affecting marine migration, we realized that some factors are not easily quantifiable, such as the amount of food available in different areas. Currently, there is no model with optimal evaluation metrics for these factors. To improve our results, we are investigating additional factors that could help refine our predictions.

So far, we have been able to create two interactive maps: one showing chlorophyll concentrations and another showing shark locations.
